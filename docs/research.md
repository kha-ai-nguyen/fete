## UK private event venue-hire TAM (and London)

The UK events industry generates about £61.6 billion annually, of which roughly £33.6 billion comes from corporate and business events such as conferences, trade shows, exhibitions and product launches. Weddings contribute a further c. £3.9 billion in **venue-side** revenue via the dedicated Wedding Venues industry, which is separately tracked.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

Event budgeting benchmarks suggest venue rental typically absorbs around 25–35% of a corporate event budget, with catering and F\&B taking a similar share. Applying that to the £33.6 billion corporate events economy implies UK corporate event venue revenue on the order of £8.4–£11.8 billion per year. Adding the c. £3.9 billion wedding venue industry and a reasonable venue component of restaurant-based private events (events are ~15–25% of restaurant revenue; room/minimum-spend makes up a significant share of that) gives a **directional UK private event venue TAM in the ~£15–25 billion range** once you include corporate events, weddings, restaurant private dining and workshops.[^5][^6][^7][^8][^9][^3][^10]

For London specifically, the Mayor’s office cites more than £12.5 billion per year added to London’s economy by the events and exhibitions industry alone. If venue hire is 25–35% of that spend, that subset implies £3.1–£4.4 billion of London venue revenue before counting restaurant private dining, London weddings and creative/production space hire. Given London and the South East capture close to 60% of UK hospitality and “party‑house” type event revenues, a **London private event venue TAM in the c. £4–7 billion range** across corporate events, weddings, private dining and workshops is a reasonable working band.[^11][^12][^8][^13][^14]

## Naboo and Perk (TravelPerk): funding and corporate event sourcing model

**Naboo**

- Funding:
    - Seed: c. €7.5m seed round in early 2024 (reported in later funding commentary; valuation not disclosed).[^15]
    - Series A: €20m in January 2025, led by Notion Capital with ISAI and other existing investors.[^16][^17][^18]
    - Series B: \$70m (≈€58.7m) Series B in February 2026 led by Lightspeed Venture Partners, again with Notion, ISAI and Ternel participating; total capital raised is ~\$99m.[^19][^20][^21]
- Scale and economics: Naboo reports booking volumes rising from €15m (2023) to €60m (2024), with a c.17% take‑rate split between suppliers (10–12%) and clients (5–6%), yielding around €10m revenue in 2024.[^22][^16]
- Business model:
    - An Airbnb‑style marketplace for corporate retreats and events, bundling venues, accommodation, catering, activities and transport.[^23][^22]
    - An enterprise SaaS layer for procurement: approvals, budgets, policies, contracts and consolidated invoicing, pitched as turning events into a governed spend category.[^24][^22][^23]
    - Monetisation primarily through commissions on GMV plus platform/SaaS fees for large accounts.

**Perk / TravelPerk**

- Funding:
    - By January 2024, TravelPerk had raised over \$520m, including a \$104m SoftBank-led extension, at around a \$1.4bn valuation.[^25][^26]
    - In January 2025 it raised a further \$200m Series E, taking valuation to about \$2.7bn and bringing cumulative funding into the high hundreds of millions (analysts estimate c. \$860m+–\$1bn across equity and debt).[^27][^28][^29]
- Business model:
    - Core: corporate travel and spend platform with SaaS tiers and margin/commission on flight, hotel and transport bookings.[^30][^31]
    - Perk Events: an AI‑powered module (Perk Events) that automates team event planning for 9–5,000 attendees, orchestrating venue selection, travel and logistics on top of the existing supplier inventory.[^32]
    - Monetisation via the same booking and subscription economics, but applied to team events and offsites as an extension of managed travel and expenses.[^26][^25]


## UK creative freelancer and studio-capacity marketplaces

There is no single published figure for “creative studio/freelancer marketplaces” in the UK, but the underlying verticals give order‑of‑magnitude guidance:

- Film and high-end TV production spending in the UK is about £6.3bn per year, supported by 6.9m sq ft of stage space, 71% of which is in London and the South East.[^11]
- UK photographic activities (professional photography and studios) generate roughly £1.9bn annually.[^33][^34]
- UK architectural services are variously estimated at about \$6.5bn to \$22.3bn in annual revenue, depending on segmentation and methodology.[^35][^36]

Global P2P rental apps are a \$17.7bn market in 2024, forecast to \$49.3bn by 2034, and the global photo studio rental segment alone is c.\$1.2bn today. Peerspace, a leading venue/studio marketplace, reports about half a billion dollars of cumulative sales and over \$110m in payouts to hosts over its lifetime, with only ~6% of traffic originating in the UK, implying that even global leaders are still at sub‑tens‑of‑billions scale.[^37][^38][^39][^40][^41]

Putting these together, the **underlying UK spend addressable by creative capacity marketplaces** (studio and location hire plus bookable architect/creative services) is easily in the low tens of billions of pounds per year, but the proportion actually flowing through online marketplaces today is almost certainly **in the low‑hundreds of millions to low single‑digit billions of GMV**, given the global numbers of comparable platforms.[^34][^40][^11]

## Early funding and pitch narratives: Fresha, Treatwell, Hussle

**Fresha (Shedul)**

- Funding milestones:
    - Series A (2017): \$6m for Shedul, described as a free booking platform for salons and spas with 40k+ merchants in 120+ countries and rapid booking growth.[^42][^43]
    - Series B (2019): \$20m at a reported \$105m valuation, led by Partech and Target Global.[^44][^45]
    - Later: \$100m Series C in 2021 led by General Atlantic; by late 2021 the company had raised c.\$214m and was valued around \$640m.[^46][^47][^48]
- Early pitch:
    - Free SaaS for beauty/wellness businesses (appointments, POS, CRM, inventory) to rapidly aggregate supply, then layer on consumer marketplace and financial services.[^43][^49]
    - Emphasis on global fragmentation and very low online penetration in bookings, with multi‑billion‑dollar booking volume targets.[^50][^49]

**Treatwell (Wahanda)**

- Funding \& valuation:
    - 2011: £3.5m investment round led by Fidelity Growth Partners Europe into Wahanda’s online health and beauty marketplace.[^51][^52]
    - 2015: Recruit Holdings, already a 10% shareholder, acquired a further 70% for £112.5m, valuing Wahanda at around \$222m.[^53][^54]
    - Wahanda acquired Dutch rival Treatwell for €34m the same year, and the combined business ultimately adopted the Treatwell brand.[^55][^56]
- Early pitch:
    - An online destination to find, discover and book health, beauty and wellness services, addressing a market where <1% of salon reservations in major European countries were online.[^57][^52]
    - Aggregating tens of thousands of salons to become the default booking layer for the category, similar to OpenTable in restaurants.[^56]

**Hussle (PayAsUGym)**

- Funding:
    - By 2013 PayAsUGym had raised about £2m, including a £900k round led by Supremum Capital.[^58]
    - 2014: additional £1.6m from Albion Ventures and MMC Ventures.[^59]
    - 2018: £6.5m Series A led by Albion Capital.[^60]
    - Aggregated data suggests total funding in the high‑teens to low‑twenties of millions of US dollars; detailed seed valuations are not public.[^61][^62]
- Early pitch:
    - An online gym network selling flexible, discounted day passes and short memberships at thousands of gyms, helping consumers avoid contracts and helping gyms monetise under‑utilised capacity.[^58][^59]
    - Later repositioned under the Hussle brand as a nationwide fitness access pass and corporate wellness benefit.[^63][^64]

Seed‑round valuations for all three companies are generally not disclosed; what is available are the amounts and valuations from Series A onward and the narratives used in contemporary coverage.

## Private dining revenue in London restaurants

Robust, London‑specific quantified data on private dining revenue per restaurant are scarce, but several industry sources give a picture of scale:

- UKHospitality benchmarking (summarised by restaurant‑operations consultants) indicates that events — including private dining, ticketed experiences and group buyouts — typically contribute around **15–25% of annual revenue** for restaurants that lean into this channel.[^5]
- Tripleseat and similar platforms report that private dining/events carry profit margins of **25–35%**, versus roughly **3–7%** for standard restaurant operations, thanks to guaranteed minimum spends, deposits and better utilisation.[^65][^66]
- London private dining rooms commonly operate on minimum spends of roughly **£600–£1,500 per event**, with an average around £1,000 according to Tagvenue’s April 2026 data; upscale central London spaces and weekend slots can be significantly higher.[^67][^6]

Taken together, these benchmarks imply that for a busy central‑London restaurant with one or more private rooms, **low‑ to mid‑six‑figure annual revenue from private events (e.g., ~£250k–£600k)** is plausible, representing **roughly 15–30% of total revenue** if the venue is actively marketed and used mid‑week as well as for peak‑time functions. The exact number depends heavily on concept, capacity, frequency of events and positioning (corporate vs social), and there is no single authoritative average published, so these figures should be used as directional bands rather than precise benchmarks.[^67][^5]
